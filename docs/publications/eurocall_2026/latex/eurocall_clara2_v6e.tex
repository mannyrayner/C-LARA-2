\begin{abstract}

C-LARA-2 is a complete reimplementation of C-LARA, an AI-based online
platform for creating multimodal language-learning materials. Because of
the widespread adoption of Generative Artificial Intelligence (Gen-AI)
coding assistants over the last year, AI-based systems in general now
often evolve faster than the academic publication cycle. We therefore
present C-LARA-2 not as a fixed collection of functionalities, but as an
experiment in organising a computer-assisted language learning (CALL)
project around limited AI autonomy. By this, we mean the following.
Human participants retain strategic responsibility for overall project
goals, pedagogical requirements, ethical constraints and acceptance
decisions; but OpenAI Codex, a popular AI coding assistant, has been
given full operational responsibility for implementation, testing and
technical documentation. Two case studies illustrate the model. In the
first, picture-dictionary functionality for minority and Indigenous
language contexts was radically redesigned after users rejected the
initial interaction model; Codex reconstructed the interface within
hours while preserving the underlying representation. In the second, an
AI Assistant with access to the code repository answers freely expressed
natural language questions by inspecting source code and AI-maintained
documentation, displaying explanations which include links to relevant
locations in the repository. We argue that C-LARA-2's main significance
lies in its development methodology. Small academic teams can delegate
much of the operational work of software construction, technical
documentation and revision while maintaining human strategic control.
Human project members thus need to spend little time addressing
requirements at the software level, allowing them to concentrate on the
higher-level challenges of project planning, user needs and verification
of system reliability.

\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} AI autonomy; computer-assisted language
learning; AI coding assistants; multimodal language learning.

\section{Introduction: describing a very rapidly evolving system}

C-LARA-2 is a new AI-based online platform for creating and using
multimodal language-learning materials. It is the successor to C-LARA, a
platform developed over several years to support the construction of
texts combining linguistic annotation, translations, audio and images,
and particularly for AI-generated pedagogical picture books tailored to
specific learners and languages (Bédi et al., 2025). C-LARA-2 keeps
these broad pedagogical goals but completely reimplements the platform,
adding a more systematic software architecture, better support for
multimodal content and a development process organised around extensive
software-level autonomy using AI coding agents. Before describing the
system and several representative areas of functionality, however, it is
useful to situate the project within a broader change in the
relationship between artificial intelligence, academic work and software
development. C-LARA-2 forms part of a rapidly expanding body of work
exploring the possibilities and limitations of generative AI in language
teaching and learning (Law, 2024).

Until recently, an academic paper about a software system could
reasonably assume that the system described in the submitted version
would be roughly the one that existed when the paper was published. Due
to the widespread introduction of AI coding agents, this assumption is
now often not valid. The rapid software development permitted by coding
agents means that the publication process cannot keep up with the speed
at which the systems change. Based on the C-LARA-2 project's evolution
so far, we expect that a detailed catalogue of the platform's July 2026
functionality would be seriously out of date by the time the article is
published in December 2026. Recent large-scale evidence confirms that
coding agents are already widely used in real-world software
development: the AIDev dataset contains more than 900,000 agent-authored
pull requests across over 100,000 GitHub repositories (Li et al., 2026).

This reflects a broader mismatch between established academic publishing
practices and AI-mediated research and development. AI systems now
contribute substantially to software, documentation, analysis and
academic writing, while the resulting division of labour is often
described inconsistently or not at all. Manabu (2026) argues that
generative AI is exposing fundamental tensions in a publication system
organised around the production of countable textual artefacts, and
large-scale studies provide strong aggregate evidence that LLM-assisted
scholarly writing has already become widespread (Kobak et al., 2025).

We do not attempt to resolve these wider questions here. Our narrower
concern is how a specific academic CALL project, C-LARA-2, can use
extensive generative AI participation productively while retaining clear
human responsibility for goals, values and evaluation. In particular,
our study addresses two closely related consequences of AI-driven
change. First, it makes AI participation explicit rather than concealing
it behind the conventional language of human authorship. Second, because
both the underlying technology and the platform itself are developing
rapidly, we deliberately avoid presenting C-LARA-2 as a completed or
stable artefact. Instead, we focus on the more general question of how
much operational responsibility can usefully be delegated to an AI, with
the overall aim of enabling a small, distributed consortium of human
users to respond quickly to new pedagogical and technical demands that
would otherwise exceed its available development capacity.

The paper consequently focuses on project goals and philosophy,
supported by a few representative episodes. Its central claim is that
C-LARA-2 is best understood as an experiment in reorganising a CALL
project around \emph{limited AI autonomy}: humans maintain strategic
responsibility for goals, priorities, values and acceptance decisions,
while an AI coding agent performs implementation, refactoring, testing,
documentation, experimental organisation and drafting. The resulting
model does not try to resolve the wider problems identified by Manabu.
It does, however, offer one concrete example of how academic work can
acknowledge extensive AI participation openly and employ it for
tangible, useful ends, while maintaining identifiable human
responsibility for the purposes and direction of the project.

\section{From C-LARA to C-LARA-2}

C-LARA, the ChatGPT-Based Learning And Reading Assistant, was developed, starting shortly after the release of ChatGPT-4 in March 2023, to support the creation of a multimodal online
language-learning environment for a wide range of languages. A typical
C-LARA project begins with an uploaded or AI-generated text and enriches
it with several interconnected annotation layers, including
segmentation, translation, lemmas, glosses, audio and images. The
resulting material can be rendered as an interactive learning resource
in which learners move between the text and its linguistic, auditory and
visual representations. The platform has been used to create several
kinds of resources, including pedagogical picture books (Bédi et al.,
2025), pronunciation-oriented texts and picture-based vocabulary
materials.

C-LARA has been used with both widely taught and low-resource languages.
Earlier work evaluated GPT-4 processing for English, Farsi, Faroese,
Mandarin and Russian (ChatGPT, C. et al., 2023), while projects
involving the Kanak languages Drehu and Iaai combined native-speaker
audio, AI-generated illustrations, linguistic annotation and culturally
grounded content (Bae et al., 2025; Chiera et al., 2025; Welby et al.,
2024). These studies do not show that AI processing works equally well
for every language\footnote{In particular, for low-resource languages
  like Drehu and Iaai, the AI processing offered by the platform is
  limited to image generation, mediated through translations provided by
  humans in an AI-comprehensible language like English or French.}, but
they demonstrate that the platform can support useful multimodal work
across varied linguistic and sociocultural settings.

The original implementation grew incrementally over several years. GPT-4
and later ChatGPT models wrote a substantial proportion of the code
under close human supervision; our informal estimate is approximately
75\%. This AI-assisted development model made it possible for a small
academic consortium to build a platform whose scope would otherwise have
required a much larger conventional software team. At the same time,
C-LARA retained many characteristics of a system that had evolved
through repeated local extensions: accumulated architectural
compromises, uneven documentation and components that had become
increasingly difficult to understand and maintain.

C-LARA-2 consequently began in November 2025 as a rational
rewrite\footnote{For a few months, work on both C-LARA and C-LARA-2
  proceeded in parallel while we compared the two systems. In March
  2026, development work on C-LARA was halted, since it had become clear
  that C-LARA-2 was far superior. At this point, about one quarter of
  the current (July 2026) C-LARA-2 repository content had been created.}.
Its initial purpose was to preserve the successful pedagogical concepts
of C-LARA while replacing the implementation with a cleaner and more
systematic architecture. The availability of Codex through the ChatGPT
Pro Tier changed the nature of the exercise. Rather than merely
assisting human coders, Codex could feasibly become responsible for the
entire implementation layer. At the time of writing, it has produced
100\% of the program code, tests and repository documentation. Human
collaborators have supplied requirements, domain knowledge, examples,
evaluation and strategic decisions, but they have not directly authored
the implementation.

This distinction is central to the project. C-LARA-2 is not simply a
conventionally written system accelerated by code completion. Program
code, tests, roadmaps, issue descriptions, technical explanations and
user-oriented documentation are maintained together in the open-source
GitHub repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/mannyrayner/C-LARA-2}.}
and can all be inspected and revised by OpenAI's Codex AI agent;
crucially, responsibility for extending and maintaining the repository
has been entirely delegated to this agent. The rewrite thus developed
into a broader experiment: not only whether an AI could generate a
complex CALL platform, but whether it could assume continuing
responsibility for the evolving technical representation of the project.

\section{Operational AI autonomy under strategic human direction}

The term \emph{autonomy} might suggest an unconstrained system pursuing
self-selected goals. That is not what we mean here. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:responsibility},
C-LARA-2 separates strategic and operational responsibility.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Indicative division of responsibility in C-LARA-2.}
\label{tab:responsibility}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{>{\bfseries}p{0.27\textwidth}X}
\toprule
Human strategic responsibility & Defining project goals; identifying user needs; setting ethical and privacy constraints; prioritising work; supplying examples and domain knowledge; assessing pedagogical usefulness; accepting, rejecting or redirecting proposed solutions; preparing drafts of technical reports and papers. \\
\midrule
AI operational responsibility & Designing and implementing code; writing tests; refactoring; producing and maintaining documentation; analysing repository state; organising experiments; processing results; suggesting and finalising technical reports and papers. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The resulting model can be summarised as \emph{operational autonomy
under strategic human direction}. The human role is closer to that of a
project owner, domain expert, evaluator and research supervisor than to
that of a coder issuing detailed line-by-line instructions. Human
participants describe the desired outcome, explain relevant constraints,
evaluate the results, and provide feedback about issues that have
arisen. Codex is responsible for translating this guidance into changes
to the technical artefacts of the project.

Codex also maintains extensive project documentation alongside the code.
The repository contains roadmaps recording the history and intended
direction of major features, how-to documents for tasks requiring human
action, and issue records tracking focused problems and ongoing work.
Together, these materials provide a persistent project memory that can
be read by both Codex and human collaborators.

Table~\ref{tab:repo-size} shows that documentation is not a minor supplement to the
software: more than 13,000 lines of the repository describe project
history, design decisions, plans and procedures. This makes it possible
for the AI to maintain continuity across a rapidly evolving platform and
for human collaborators to understand and redirect the work. The figures
are a transient snapshot rather than a measure of software quality.

The approach depends on safeguards. All work is version-controlled.
Changes can be inspected, tested and rolled back. Agent access is
limited according to task: the public Assistant described below is only
permitted to read information from the repository (`read-only sandbox'),
while code-changing workflows are constrained to designated repository
locations. Automated tests do not guarantee correctness, but they reduce
the cost of repeated revision and provide a persistent representation of
agreed behaviour. Human review remains necessary, especially for
learner-facing content and culturally sensitive material.

A major issue, still only partially resolved, is the \emph{verification
burden}. AI agents can produce code and text faster than humans can
examine them. The bottleneck therefore shifts from production to
specification, sampling, testing and judgement. High autonomy is useful
only when the project also develops mechanisms that make outputs
inspectable, project history recoverable and failures reversible. In
C-LARA-2, the repository documentation, issue structure, tests and
version-control history are intended to provide that supporting
infrastructure.

\section{Case study 1: picture dictionaries and rapid redesign}

A first case study arose from requests by consortium members working
with minority and Indigenous language communities. Picture-based
resources can be particularly valuable when learners have limited
literacy, when an orthography is still being consolidated, or when
community members wish to create accessible materials for young
learners. This case is pedagogically central because it concerns not
only software efficiency, but whether community members can
realistically author and review learning materials themselves.

The initial design built on the existing C-LARA-2 representation in a
natural way. A picture dictionary was represented as an ordinary
C-LARA-2 project whose text contained one lexical item on each page,
which allowed the picture-dictionary functionality to reuse the
platform's existing text, image and project-management infrastructure.
C-LARA-2 already supported page-level image creation: an AI-generated
prompt could be derived from the text on a page and passed to an
image-generation model, with the resulting image stored as part of the
project (Rendina et al., 2025).

The underlying representation proved successful, but the first user
interface did not. Ordinary C-LARA-2 projects typically contain extended
texts, with linguistic annotation and image generation presented as
separate activities. This division is appropriate when pages contain
complex text and the project language can itself be processed by an AI.
A picture dictionary is a much more constrained case: each page contains
a single word or lexicalised expression, and the relevant linguistic and
visual information is tightly connected. Moreover, many of the intended
users are minority language community contributors rather than technical
specialists, and the project language may not be understood reliably, or
at all, by the available AI systems.

Applying the general project interface to this specialised task
consequently introduced complexity without providing a corresponding
benefit. Users had to move between separate annotation and
image-generation workflows in order to manage information that
conceptually belonged to a single dictionary entry. Early testing showed
that this interaction model was difficult to understand and cumbersome
to use.

Following discussion with the intended users, we retained the underlying
representation of a picture dictionary as a C-LARA-2 project but
replaced the user-experience layer with an interface designed
specifically for dictionary construction. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:picture-dictionary-editor},
the revised editor displays one integrated block for each lexical item.
The block contains the word, its lemma and part of speech, a gloss or
translation in a language that the AI can process, optional human
guidance, the (initially AI-generated) prompt supplied to the
image-generation model, and the currently selected image. Controls allow
contributors to request missing translations, prompts or images for
multiple selected entries while keeping the ability to inspect and edit
each component manually.

Codex produced a usable implementation of the redesigned interface
within a few hours, and the consortium members involved in testing
responded very positively.\footnote{A caveat is in order: the relevant
  consortium members were less positive about the image generation
  process itself, and stressed that more work was required here. We
  postpone further discussion to a later paper.} The episode is
significant because the revised system was not a minor visual
improvement to the first design. It embodied a different conceptual
model of the task. The original implementation treated
picture-dictionary construction as a sequence of already existing
C-LARA-2 operations; the replacement treated the dictionary entry itself
as the unit around which all relevant operations should be organised.

This illustrates a broader consequence of AI-mediated development: it
reduces the human time and organisational effort required to abandon a
poor implementation while preserving useful underlying components.
Conventional software projects are strongly affected by the familiar
sunk-cost effect, in which prior investment of time or effort creates
pressure to continue with an existing approach (Arkes \& Blumer, 1985).
In this case, however, once the human participants recognised that the
interaction model was wrong, Codex made it practical to throw away and
reconstruct the user-facing layer almost immediately.

The episode also clarifies the respective roles of human and AI
autonomy. The AI did not independently recognise the community need or
identify the usability failure. Human collaborators supplied the
pedagogical purpose, knowledge of the intended users and the judgement
that the first design was inappropriate. Codex provided rapid
operational execution once the revised conceptual model had been agreed.
The successful outcome therefore depended not on replacing human
expertise, but on making it possible to translate that expertise into a
radically revised implementation with very little additional development
time.

The same picture-dictionary entries are already reused in several
learner-facing activities. In picture glossing, clicking a word or
expression in a C-LARA-2 text displays its picture, plays its audio and
shows concordance examples. The platform also creates multiple-choice
exercises in both directions---picture to word and word to
picture---with distractors selected by the AI model, together with word
scrambles and simple crosswords using picture clues. Codex produced
useful initial versions of all four functions rapidly, illustrating how
a single community-authored resource can support several forms of
multimodal practice. Figure~\ref{fig:word-scramble} shows a partially completed word-scramble
exercise generated from a French picture-dictionary.

\section{Case study 2: a project that can explain itself}

Our second case study is the C-LARA-2 Assistant. Through the online
platform's Assistant tab, users can ask unconstrained natural language
questions about C-LARA-2 itself\footnote{So far, most of our testing has
  used questions submitted in English. Based on a few samples, however,
  it seems that queries can also be submitted in other major languages,
  with equally good results. The Assistant responds in the same
  language.}. Codex answers by accessing the current repository in a
read-only environment. Its references to relevant files and line numbers
are converted into clickable links, allowing users to inspect the
evidence behind the answer.

The Assistant is not a conventional FAQ system or a manually maintained
database of answers. It retrieves information directly from the GitHub
repository using the same AI system that develops and maintains it. Over
many months of intensive use, we have found its retrieval of relevant
files and code locations highly reliable. Questions include whether a
capability is supported and how to use it, the status of a planned
feature or known problem, and the reasons behind a design decision.

Figure~\ref{fig:mandarin-pinyin} shows a representative interaction. The user asks whether the
platform can produce Mandarin texts with pinyin\footnote{`Pinyin' refers
  to the practice of annotating Chinese characters with Roman letters,
  normally placed directly above the characters, to show pronunciation.}
annotations; the Assistant consults several separate parts of the
repository: language choices and project models, the Mandarin
text-generation prompt, tokenisation code, automatic and manual pinyin
processing, conversion to HTML, and dependencies on other software
packages.

Figure~\ref{fig:haitian-audio} illustrates a different kind of answer. A user asks whether
native-speaker audio can be recorded directly in the platform for
Haitian Creole, because current text-to-speech output is unsuitable. The
Assistant correctly distinguishes what is implemented from what is
merely planned: it identifies the present option for suppressing
synthetic audio, then links to documents describing planned browser
recording and community approval workflows. The example shows why the
AI-maintained documentation matters pedagogically: the Assistant can
give a useful and honest answer even when the requested function is not
yet available.

Answering such questions may require several kinds of evidence. Source
code and tests describe current behaviour; roadmap files record the
history and intended direction of larger areas of work; issue files
track focused tasks and unresolved problems; and how-to documents
describe operations that need to be carried out by human project
members. An answer can therefore reflect not only what the software
does, but why it was designed that way and what remains incomplete.

In a deliberately limited functional sense, the project can thus be said
to \emph{understand itself}: it contains an interface through which an
AI can inspect the artefacts constituting the project and explain their
relationships to users and developers. We make no claim about
consciousness or human-like comprehension. The claim is operational: the
system can construct useful, context-sensitive accounts of its own
current implementation, history and planned development.

 The Assistant cannot be perfect. Documentation may lag behind code,
record an abandoned intention, or omit a relevant relationship; a
response can also be accurate without being optimally helpful. Its
answers should therefore be treated as informed, inspectable accounts
rather than definitive statements. Nevertheless, when code, tests,
documentation, roadmaps and issues are accessible to the same agent, the
repository becomes more than a container for software. It is in fact
more accurate to think of it as a project memory accessible both to the
AI and to the human participants.

\section{Further directions: integration with spoken conversational functionalities}

As already noted, C-LARA-2 is evolving rapidly, and we expect many new
functionalities to have been added by the time this paper is published.
One specific point is central enough that it needs to be mentioned
explicitly. Current systems still separate modalities awkwardly.
C-LARA-2 can create multimodal texts, and modern voice systems like
ChatGPT Voice Mode can sustain increasingly sophisticated spoken
conversational interaction, but these capabilities are not integrated in
a natural way. Ideally, a learner should be able to open a C-LARA-2
text, discuss it in Voice Mode, and move flexibly between the written
material and the spoken interaction. This direction is consistent with a
growing CALL literature on voice-based AI chatbots and their potential
to support spoken language practice (Koç \& Savaş, 2025).

Experiments reported in another paper we are presenting at EuroCALL 2026
suggest that much of the required infrastructure already exists. A
primitive integration can be achieved now by opening C-LARA-2 and
ChatGPT Pro Tier in separate browser windows, uploading a screenshot of
the current C-LARA-2 page to ChatGPT and then switching to Voice Mode.
This workflow is obviously too cumbersome for practical use, but the
quality of the resulting interaction suggests that a smooth integration
would require comparatively modest interface engineering rather than
substantial architecture changes.

\section{Summary, discussion and conclusions}

The significance of C-LARA-2 is not simply that AI can help coders work
faster. The deeper change is that CALL platforms can become continuously
adaptable rather than functioning as relatively fixed products. Teachers
and researchers may increasingly be able to express requirements in
ordinary language, after which an AI agent modifies code, prompts,
workflows, interfaces, tests and documentation. For small languages and
specialised learner populations, this is particularly important, as
features needed by these communities may be impossible to justify within
a conventional development model. By greatly reducing the human
development time and organisational effort required for implementation
and revision, AI-assisted development can make highly specialised
functionality realistic. The picture-dictionary work offers an early
example, and in particular shows that a flawed interaction model can be
discarded without prohibitive loss of human development time.

C-LARA-2 also makes the division of labour unusually explicit: Codex
writes and maintains the repository, while human collaborators define
pedagogical goals, evaluate results and retain final responsibility.
This transparency is important, but the main educational significance
lies in the capacity to respond rapidly to learner and community needs.
More generally, AI changes the relationship between implementation and
educational design. The same agent that writes a feature can document it
and answer questions about it, while human collaborators can redirect
any stage of the process.

C-LARA-2 therefore represents more than a rewrite of an existing CALL
platform. It is an experiment in transferring operational responsibility
to an AI agent while humans retain strategic responsibility for goals,
values, evaluation and acceptance. By the time this paper appears, many
implementation details will have changed. That is part of the argument:
the project should be understood not as a finished artefact, but as
evidence for an emerging mode of CALL development that is strategically
human-directed, operationally AI-autonomous, continuously documented and
capable of rapid response to new needs.

\section*{Using C-LARA-2}

C-LARA-2 is openly available at \url{https://c-lara-2.c-lara.org/}.
Creating a user account is free. To use the AI-based functions, users
must connect an OpenAI API key associated with a valid payment method
through the ``Use my OpenAI API key'' control in the Profile tab.

The C-LARA-2 platform has been developed in a close collaboration
